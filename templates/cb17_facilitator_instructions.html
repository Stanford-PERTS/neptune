When talking with students, DON’T emphasize that this program is meant to help them.

WHY? Doing so can actually interfere with the program’s ability to help students. Sometimes people get defensive when they are told something is meant to help them. They may feel insulted if they think others think they need help. They could become unduly skeptical, which could prevent them from taking in the key ideas of the program. For this reason, please do not refer to the program as “{{ program_name }}” when speaking with students. Instead, you can describe the program as a welcome activity to better understand how students learn and what colleges can do to make the transition better for future students.

Suggested language: “This activity gives students the opportunity to learn more about students’ experiences coming to college and to share their own perspective on the transition. We hope to use your insights to help improve the transition for future students.”

If students ask why they should complete the writing activity, DO emphasize that they are being asked to complete it because your school wants to hear their feedback and know about them.

Suggested language: “As students currently in the transition to college, you know first-hand about the challenges you’re facing. We want to learn more from you and your classmates about what the transition to our campus is like. We hope to share your experiences with future students to help them with their transition to college. In addition, most students who have completed this activity in the past have told us that they enjoyed reading stories from students who have gone through the transition, and that they learned something from doing so.”

Instructions for Independent Completion Over Summer

Please review these instructions if your school plans to deliver the program to students over the summer to complete on their own, before they arrive on campus.

Instructions

At a high level, implementation will look as follows. This document will go in-depth about your options for each step.

  1. Decide how you’ll want to invite students to participate. There are a number of options available, and we encourage you to use a combination of invitation methods to increase the likelihood that students complete the module.
  2. Decide when you’ll begin inviting students to participate and what deadlines you’ll set. Typically we recommend schools begin reaching out to students at least a month before they’ll arrive on campus and set an initial deadline for participation at least two weeks prior to arrival. That way you can extend the deadline as needed, if response rates are lower than desired.
  3. Invite students to participate! Depending on what you chose in Step 1, this could mean sending email invitations, distributing a new student checklist that includes an invitation link, adding an invitation link to a routine online portal used by your students, and/or other methods used at your school. Most schools find that using a combination of multiple invitation methods results in the highest participation rates from students.
  4. Send reminder emails as needed until your school’s final deadline. Please coordinate with your college liaison for this program if you’re not sure when that date is. After that date, the program will no longer be accessible by anyone.

Invitation Methods

There are many ways schools have invited students to the Social-Belonging program in prior implementations. The following list is not mutually exclusive, and we have found in prior implementations that schools see the highest participation rates when using a combination of these methods to invite students:

  • New student orientation checklists: Many schools already provide their students a checklist of suggested or required activities as part of new student orientation; this may be delivered in emails, in print, in an online portal (e.g., Blackboard, Moodle), or some combination of these channels. We highly suggest schools include this program in checklist documents or portals/dashboards whenever possible. Schools that have done so in the past typically saw significantly higher response rates.
  • Web portals and dashboards: Many schools have online portals or dashboards (e.g., Blackboard, Moodle) where students routinely log in to view forms, calendars, and information on other requirements. The program can be included as a module in this location. As noted above, if your school hosts a new student orientation checklist through one of these portals/dashboards, we recommend the program be included on that checklist.
  • Direct invitation emails: We highly suggest sending emails specifically inviting students to the program, using your institution’s standard mail merge procedures. You can find suggested language and invitation timelines in the Facilitator Instructions section.
  • Newsletters and other aggregated communications: Some schools send regular emails to their incoming students, such as weekly newsletters. You can find suggested language to include in this document.
  • Other strategies as appropriate for your school: Your school may have other resources available to invite students, and we encourage you to consider what other methods may be effective at your school. For example, some schools have used phone banks to successfully increase student participation in the past.

Language and Tips: Embedded Invitation (e.g. Checklist, Online Dashboards, Printed Materials)

In previous implementations, schools have invited students to the program through orientation checklists for new students (either online or in print), in dashboards students routinely access (e.g., Blackboard, Moodle), in email newsletters to students, and in printed materials mailed to students over the summer. Generally, including the program in as many orientation materials as possible as a required or encouraged activity increases student participation.

If your school plans to embed the link to the Social-Belonging Program in an orientation checklist, an online portal, or other student-facing materials, we recommend:

  • Including the program alongside other mandatory, important items
  • Listing the program near the top or middle of the page or list
  • Describing the program in a way that seems inviting and not excessively time-consuming

Language and Tips: Email Invitation

In addition to other invitation methods (e.g., checklists, dashboards, printed materials), we highly suggest sending students email invitations using the mail merge methods your institution uses most commonly. Emails are most effective when:

  • They address students by name in the salutation.
  • There are 2-5 email reminders sent. Too few usually results in few students doing the activity. More than five emails tends not to produce more response from students, but may make them feel inundated with emails.
  • Emails come from either someone with authority, indicating the importance of the activity (i.e. vice provost, dean) OR someone students may know personally, indicating relevance to their life (i.e. resident advisor, professor, student life administrator) OR both.
  • Emails are concise and prominently display the link to the activity.
  • Emails are sent to students’ personal email addresses rather than their college email addresses, as many students do not start using their college addresses before arriving on campus.

Email invitations should originate from the relevant office at your school (i.e., the office you specified in customizing the program materials in your Dashboard). Please refer to the Invitation Timeline section of this document for a suggested email timeline.

Here are some sample email invitations you can use. These include both the initial email invitation as well as subsequent follow-up reminders as needed. Please customize as needed for your school context, but make sure not to deviate from how the activity is described. The activity should be described to participating students as a way to learn more about coming to college, as an opportunity to share their thoughts and feelings about the transition to college, and as an opportunity to help future students. The activity should NOT be described as an attempt to help, intervene, or experiment upon the participating students.

Response Rate Strategies

Your PERTS Dashboard includes real-time tracking of how many students are participating in the program. While response rates are a helpful indicator of how effective your invitation methods are, they also vary based on other factors. For instance, we find that smaller schools often find it easier to reach a larger proportion of their students than larger schools. It may be helpful to use typical response rates to campus-wide initiatives at your school as a reference point for determining if your response rate is high, low, or what you would expect.

Response Rate Too Low

If you notice your response rates are significantly lower than you expect relative to how long the program has been available to students, you may consider adding additional invitation methods or increasing the degree of importance you communicate in your invitations (e.g., “Your participation in this activity is very important to us”).

Extended Deadline and Last Chance Emails

We recommend setting the initial deadline communicated to students at least 15 days prior to students arrival on campus (see Table 1. Suggested invitation timeline). When you reach that initial deadline, you can review your current completion rate and choose to extend the deadline by an extra week if you feel your response rates are low. We have found that “deadline extended” and “last chance” emails can be effective in boosting participation rates.

Instructions for Supervised Completion During Orientation Week

Please review these instructions if your school plans to deliver the program during in-person, supervised sessions during new student orientation, while students are on campus.

Instructions

Step 1

Please schedule a time to give students access to computers with an Internet connection—either by bringing them to a computer lab or by requesting a laptop cart.

Please keep in mind that the program takes 30 minutes for students to complete.

Step 2

Obtain a student roster with student IDs. Students will need to enter their ID numbers during the activity, and some may not remember them. Please verify that each student has their ID number, and look up IDs with your roster if needed.

Step 3

Please read the following script to students before they begin.

“As a part of [SCHOOL TITLE FOR NEW STUDENT ORIENTATION], we would like you to complete the ‘What is it like coming to {{ organization.name }}?’ online activity. This is an activity that all incoming first-year students at {{ organization.name }} are being asked to complete before they start classes in the fall. Because we think that this is a really useful activity for students to complete, we are offering it as a part of your orientation.

“The activity will share stories from current and former college students describing what their experience coming to college was like. You will have a chance to share your own thoughts and experiences about coming to {{ organization.name }} to help improve the transition to college for future students.

“It will take about 30 minutes to complete the activity. Please read the instructions carefully and do your best to answer all of the questions to the best of your ability. It is very important that you not talk to each other or look at each other’s answers, as that would be a violation of other students’ privacy.

“You will be answering survey questions, reading some short passages, and answering questions about what you read. Your responses will be kept confidential. They will only be used to help our college learn about things we can do to make the transition to college better for future students. You can learn more at perts.net/privacy.

Step 4

Write the website address and the participation code where all your students can see it. If this is not possible, you may want to make cards with this information on it to hand out to your students. It is important to not let students see the website address and participation code until you read the script above so that they pay attention while you are introducing the program.

Please read the following:

“Now open your browser and please go to comingtocollege.org. Then enter the phrase {{ project_cohort.code }}, and follow the on-screen instructions. Please be aware that as you move through the program, you may want to go back to a previous question. If so, do not use the back arrow on the browser. Instead, use the ‘Go Back’ link in the upper left corner, just above the questions. Please begin.

Step 5

Ten minutes before students need to finish, tell them how much time is remaining and that it is okay if they do not have the time to finish everything.

“We have about ten minutes remaining for the activity. Please continue with the activity, but do not rush. It is okay if you do not have time to finish everything.

Step 6

At the end of the activity, ask your students to log out by closing their browser window.

“We have reached the end of the session. It’s okay if you are not totally done with the activity, just close the browser. You can complete the activity in your own time. To complete the activity later, please go to comingtocollege.org, enter the code {{ project_cohort.code }}, and type in your student ID. The website should bring you back to the page you left off at. Please take note of the website and code now. Thank you. The information you have provided may be used to improve students’ experiences here and at other schools as well.

Troubleshooting

We work hard to minimize the technical problems, but sometimes issues come up. Here are some problems students have experienced in the past and the best ways to solve them.

Error Message: Confirm Form Resubmission

Problem: A student clicked the back button on the browser.
Solution: Click the “reload” or “refresh” button. You may also need to click “confirm” or “continue.”

Closed Page

Problem: A student closed the browser window.
Solution: Tell the student to go to the website and sign in again, just like they did the first time. The program will continue where the student left off.

Warning Message or Connection Expired Message

Problem: A student's screen says that the connection expired or a similar warning.
Solution: Click the “reload” or “refresh” button.

Lost Location

Problem: The survey restarted from the beginning.
Solution: Tell the student to skip up to the part where they left off. The answers the student previously provided have been saved, but the server lost the page they were on for network reasons.

Note: Some pages may not allow students to advance until a certain amount of time (about 5 seconds) has passed.

Frequently Asked Student Questions and Responses

  • Do I have to do this?
    Your opinions are important and help us better understand what the transition to college is like so we can work to improve the experience for other students in the future. This activity is not required, but we hope you’ll do your best to complete it.
  • Why is this activity important?
    This activity is important because it allows us to better understand what the transition to college is like so we can work to improve the experience for other students in the future. Your perspective is very important to us as we try to understand our students and help future students.
  • Will I get a grade on this?
    No, the activity is not graded. Your honest answers will help us improve the transition to college for other students in the future.
  • Does spelling and grammar matter?
    No, just concentrate on getting your ideas across.
  • How long ago did the survey that these stories are from happen
    The student stories were collected and updated over the past couple of years.
  • Will I be asked to share my story (what I wrote) during orientation?
    No, this is just an independent activity to allow you to read other students’ stories and to reflect on your own experience. We will not be discussing the activity during orientation.
  • Were my responses submitted?
    Your responses are saved as you go. If you complete the activity and the internet cuts out or you try to submit and it doesn’t look like it worked, your responses are already saved in the system and you do not need to re-submit.
  • I started the activity but didn’t have time to finish. Can I keep going where I left off
    The activity saves your response. If you click on the link to sign in again, you should be able to resume from the place where you stopped. All of your previous responses were saved.
  • The next button isn’t working!
    On some pages, there is a delay with the next button in order to give students time to read the information on the page without feeling rushed. It will appear at the bottom of the page after a short delay.
  • Why isn’t there a back button?
    We don’t include a back button because we want to encourage students to respond to the questions in the moment rather than going back and changing them. If you accidentally clicked the next button or want to change your previous responses you will not be able to, but that is okay.
  • Do I have to share my personal information (name, student ID, etc.)?
    Yes, it is necessary in order to participate. Be assured, your personal information will be kept secure and confidential in accordance with the Privacy Policy.
  • Why do you ask for demographic information?
    We ask for demographic information so that we can understand more about our student population and the students participating in the activity. Your responses to these questions will be kept secure.
  • I am visually impaired. Will this activity work with my text reader?
    Yes.
  • I am hearing impaired. Is there audio in the activity that I am missing?
    This activity does not include any audio.
